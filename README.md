Abstract

In this project, our team analyzed National Park Service data to determine how season and region influenced how long people spend at national parks in the United States. We analyzed monthly recreation visitor hours and recreation visits from 2004 - 2024, and used it to look at seasonal and regional trends. We found that visitors tend to spend the fewest hours per visit, and visit less during the winter months, an effect that is present across all regions, but exacerbated in the Pacific Northwest parks, where visitors seem to flock in the summer. We also found that parks are not equally distributed in their average length of visitation, and their number of visits. Some parks recieve the vast majority of total visits. There are some parks that tend to have more shorter visits, and others that favor longer, but fewer visits. While we found a lot of interesting data trends, the diverse nature of the parks and the nature of some park's popularity and unique weather conditions causing hazards may limit our findings.
